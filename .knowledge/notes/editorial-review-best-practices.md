# Editorial Review Best Practices

**Purpose:** Practical guidance for conducting four types of content reviews—structural, content, style, and general—based on authoritative sources including the Chicago Manual of Style, Purdue OWL, and technical writing standards.

---

## 1. Structural Review

**Purpose:** Evaluate narrative arc, logical flow, organization, transitions, paragraph structure, and how main points connect.

### What to Look For

#### Narrative Arc & Logical Flow
- Does the piece have a clear beginning, middle, and end?
- Is there a discernible progression of ideas?
- Does each section build on the previous one?
- Are there any abrupt shifts or gaps in logic?
- Does the conclusion effectively summarize and resolve the narrative arc?

#### Organization
- Is the overall structure appropriate for the content type (chronological, problem-solution, cause-effect, compare-contrast)?
- Are main points distributed evenly across sections?
- Is the introduction effective (sets context, states purpose, previews content)?
- Does the conclusion do more than just repeat the introduction?
- Are headings and subheadings clear and appropriately nested?

#### Transitions
- Do paragraphs link to each other logically?
- Are there transitional phrases or sentences between sections?
- Does each paragraph end with a bridge to the next?
- Are there signpost phrases that guide readers through the argument?

#### Paragraph Structure
- Does each paragraph have a clear topic sentence?
- Are supporting details sufficient and relevant?
- Do paragraphs maintain unity (one main idea each)?
- Is paragraph length appropriate for the content and audience?
- Is there appropriate white space and visual breaks?

### Structural Review Checklist

- [ ] Clear thesis or purpose statement in introduction
- [ ] Logical progression of main points
- [ ] Each paragraph focused on single main idea
- [ ] Effective transitions between paragraphs and sections
- [ ] Appropriate use of headings and subheadings
- [ ] Conclusion summarizes and provides closure
- [ ] No gaps in logic or missing supporting information
- [ ] Balance of section lengths
- [ ] Reader can follow the argument without confusion
- [ ] Structure appropriate for intended purpose and audience

### Patterns to Avoid

- **Circular reasoning** — returning to the same point without progression
- **Gaps in logic** — jumping from one idea to another without bridging
- **Buried thesis** — main point hidden deep in the document
- **Structural imbalance** — one section disproportionately large or small
- **Orphan paragraphs** — paragraphs that don't connect to surrounding content
- **Dead-end transitions** — transitions that don't lead anywhere meaningful
- **Topic sentence missing** — paragraph without a clear central idea

## 2. Content Review

**Purpose:** Assess factuality, methodology soundness, argument strength, evidence quality, and completeness.

### What to Look For

#### Factuality & Accuracy
- Are all factual claims verifiable?
- Are statistics and numbers accurate and sourced?
- Are dates, names, and proper nouns correct?
- Is there any outdated or potentially obsolete information?
- Are technical specifications or data current?

#### Methodology Soundness (for research/analysis)
- Is the methodology clearly stated?
- Is the methodology appropriate for the research question?
- Are limitations acknowledged?
- Is the sample size/data set adequate?
- Are assumptions explicitly stated?

#### Argument Strength
- Does the thesis make a clear, defensible claim?
- Are counterarguments addressed?
- Is the argument proportionate to the evidence?
- Are conclusions logically derived from premises?
- Is there any logical fallacy present?

#### Evidence Quality
- Is evidence specific and concrete rather than vague?
- Are sources credible and authoritative?
- Is evidence recent enough for the topic?
- Are quotes and citations accurately reproduced?
- Is there sufficient evidence for each major claim?
- Are visualizations (charts, graphs) accurate and labeled correctly?

#### Completeness
- Are all aspects of the topic addressed?
- Are prerequisites or background information provided?
- Is the scope clearly defined (and followed)?
- Are key terms defined for the audience?
- Is there anything the reader would need that isn't provided?

### Content Review Checklist

- [ ] All facts are accurate and verifiable
- [ ] Statistics and data are correctly sourced and cited
- [ ] Methodology (if applicable) is sound and appropriate
- [ ] Claims are supported by sufficient, credible evidence
- [ ] Evidence directly supports the arguments made
- [ ] Counterarguments are acknowledged and addressed
- [ ] Limitations and assumptions are stated
- [ ] Scope is clear and consistent throughout
- [ ] All key terms are defined for the target audience
- [ ] Nothing essential is missing; nothing extraneous included
- [ ] Citations follow the required style guide
- [ ] Sources are current and authoritative

### Patterns to Avoid

- **Unsubstantiated claims** — assertions without supporting evidence
- **False balance** — giving equal weight to opposing views despite evidence disparity
- **Anecdotal evidence overreach** — generalizing from single examples
- **Citation artifacts** — claims that were true in cited source but outdated now
- **Scope creep** — addressing topics beyond the stated scope
- **Narrow framing** — presenting complex issues as binary
- **Appeal to authority** — using authority figures rather than evidence
- **Hasty generalization** — drawing broad conclusions from limited data

## 3. Style Review

**Purpose:** Examine grammar, wording, spelling, tone, voice consistency, clarity, and readability.

### What to Look For

#### Grammar & Mechanics
- Subject-verb agreement
- Pronoun reference and agreement
- Consistent tense usage
- Parallel structure in lists and comparisons
- Correct use of modifiers
- Complete sentences (no fragments or run-ons)
- Correct comma usage and other punctuation

#### Wording & Vocabulary
- Word choice appropriate for audience and purpose
- Consistent use of terminology
- Avoidance of jargon (unless appropriate for audience)
- Precise word selection (no vague language)
- Elimination of redundancy
- Active voice predominance

#### Spelling & Mechanics
- Spelling accuracy (including homonyms)
- Consistent capitalization
- Correct number formatting (words vs. figures)
- Proper hyphenation
- Correct abbreviation usage

#### Tone & Voice
- Tone appropriate for purpose (persuasive, informative, neutral)
- Consistent level of formality
- No mixed signals about audience
- Voice is consistent (not shifting between first/second/third person)
- Point of view consistent throughout

#### Clarity & Readability
- Sentences not overly long or complex
- Paragraphs not too dense
- Technical concepts explained when needed
- Acronyms spelled out on first use
- Lists and examples break up dense text
- Readability level appropriate for audience

### Style Review Checklist

**Grammar & Mechanics**
- [ ] No sentence fragments or run-ons
- [ ] No comma splices
- [ ] Subject-verb agreement correct
- [ ] Pronouns clearly refer to antecedents
- [ ] Verb tenses consistent
- [ ] Parallel structure in lists and comparisons

**Word Usage**
- [ ] Words used precisely and correctly
- [ ] Consistent terminology throughout
- [ ] No redundant phrases
- [ ] Active voice used appropriately
- [ ] Jargon explained or avoided

**Spelling & Mechanics**
- [ ] No spelling errors (including homonyms)
- [ ] Punctuation consistent and correct
- [ ] Numbers formatted consistently
- [ ] Capitalization correct

**Tone & Voice**
- [ ] Tone appropriate for audience and purpose
- [ ] Voice consistent throughout
- [ ] No abrupt shifts in tone or style
- [ ] Professional/proper distance maintained

**Clarity**
- [ ] Sentences clear and not overly complex
- [ ] Technical terms defined or accessible
- [ ] Acronyms introduced on first use
- [ ] Paragraphs not too dense
- [ ] Formatting aids readability

### Common Grammar Issues to Flag

| Issue | Example | Correction |
|-------|---------|------------|
| **Subject-verb disagreement** | "The team are ready" | "The team is ready" |
| **Pronoun ambiguity** | "Sam and Alex argued. She was wrong." | Specify who "she" refers to |
| **Mixed construction** | "Since I was tired is why I left" | "Since I was tired, I left" |
| **Dangling modifier** | "Walking to school, the tree fell on me" | "Walking to school, I saw a tree fall" |
| **Parallelism error** | "The job involves testing, fixing, and to document" | "testing, fixing, and documenting" |

### Patterns to Avoid

- **Passive voice overuse** — weakens prose and obscures agency
- **Nominalization** — using nouns where verbs would be stronger
- **Hedging** — excessive qualification that weakens arguments
- **Clichés** — overused phrases that reduce credibility
- **Weasel words** — vague qualifiers that undermine claims
- **Filler words** — "very," "really," "basically" that add nothing
- **Mixed metaphors** — combining incompatible figurative language

## 4. General Review

**Purpose:** Form overall impression, evaluate cohesion across all dimensions, and assess how well the piece achieves its purpose.

### What to Look For

#### Overall Cohesion
- Do all elements work together as a unified whole?
- Is there consistent voice and style throughout?
- Does the content match the promised scope?
- Are there any jarring shifts in tone, depth, or approach?
- Does the piece feel complete—not too short or padded?

#### Purpose Achievement
- Will the piece achieve its intended goal?
- Is the call to action (if any) clear and compelling?
- Is the key takeaway memorable?
- Does the conclusion make an impact?
- Will the target audience find this useful/relevant?

#### Reader Experience
- Is it engaging from the start?
- Does it hold attention throughout?
- Is it easy to navigate (find information, follow argument)?
- Is the visual presentation professional?
- Would a reader recommend this piece?

#### Consistency Check
- Terminology consistent
- Tone consistent
- Citation style consistent
- Formatting consistent
- Voice consistent
- Level of detail consistent

### General Review Checklist

- [ ] Document achieves its stated purpose
- [ ] Target audience would find it useful and accessible
- [ ] Voice and tone consistent throughout
- [ ] All sections cohesive and interconnected
- [ ] Introduction hooks reader and sets expectations
- [ ] Conclusion memorable and actionable
- [ ] No obvious gaps or redundancies
- [ ] Formatting professional and consistent
- [ ] Visual elements (if any) effective
- [ ] Would engage the intended audience
- [ ] Quality matches or exceeds comparable publications

### Patterns to Avoid

- **Incohesive sections** — parts that could be extracted without losing meaning
- **Mixed signals** — formal tone with casual content, or vice versa
- **Tone deafness** — inappropriate tone for audience or purpose
- **Padded content** — unnecessary words or sections to reach length
- **Thin content** — insufficient depth for the scope
- **Uneven quality** — excellent sections followed by weak ones
- **Broken promises** — introduction promises not delivered in body

## Review Process Recommendations

### Review Order
1. **Structural review first** — Fix organization before investing time in details
2. **Content review second** — Ensure accuracy and logic before polishing prose
3. **Style review third** — Address grammar, wording, and tone once structure and content are solid
4. **General review last** — Step back and assess the whole piece

### Practical Tips
- **Read aloud** — Catches awkward phrasing, run-ons, and missing words
- **Print it out** — Screen reading misses errors visible on paper
- **Read backwards** — Forces focus on individual sentences rather than meaning
- **Use search** — Find repeated words, phrases, or consistency issues
- **Let it rest** — Time between writing and reviewing improves objectivity
- **Fresh eyes** — A second reviewer catches what familiarity obscures

### When to Flag vs. Fix
- **Flag:** Major structural issues, factuality concerns, weak arguments
- **Fix (or suggest):** Typos, minor grammar, awkward phrasing, formatting
- **Discuss:** Borderline cases, style guide exceptions, tone considerations

---

## Quick Reference: Red Flags to Always Flag

- Factual errors or unverifiable claims
- Logical fallacies in arguments
- Missing source citations for data/quotes
- Broken internal links or references
- Inconsistent terminology
- Mixed citation styles
- Pronoun reference ambiguity
- Repeated words or phrases
- Buried or missing thesis
- Unresolved contradictions
- Formatting inconsistencies
- Acronyms used without introduction
